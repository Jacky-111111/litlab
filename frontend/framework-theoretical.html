Theoretical Paper

Write a Theoretical paper

A theoretical paper advances an idea — a new claim, model, or framework — and justifies it through structured reasoning and prior evidence. You are making an argument, not reporting an experiment.

When to use

You have a new conceptual contribution to propose.

Typical length

5,000 – 8,000 words

Key move

State a bold claim, then defend it step by step.

The big idea

A theoretical paper is a chain of reasoning: you identify a problem in how the field currently thinks, propose a different way to think about it, and show why your way holds up. Your evidence is other papers, logic, and sometimes formal models — not new data.

Section-by-section walkthrough

  1. 1

    Problem & Motivation

    Goal: Show that the current way of thinking is incomplete, inconsistent, or missing something important.

    What to include

    • A phenomenon the field struggles to explain.
    • A puzzle, tension, or contradiction in prior theory.
    • Why solving this matters — to research or practice.

    Writing tip: the reader should feel dissatisfied with the status quo by the end of this section. If they don't, they won't care about your new model.

  2. 2

    Existing Theories

    Goal: Summarize the main theoretical positions fairly before you critique them.

    What to include

    • Two or three major existing views.
    • What each gets right.
    • Where each falls short with respect to your problem.

    Writing tip: steel-man the competing theories. A paper that mocks its rivals is easy to dismiss.

  3. 3

    Proposed Claim or Model

    Goal: State your new idea precisely.

    What to include

    • A one-sentence central claim.
    • Core constructs and how they relate (a diagram helps).
    • Assumptions and the boundary conditions.

    Writing tip: if you cannot state your claim in one sentence, it is not yet a claim — it is a vibe. 

    Example claim
"We propose that attention in classroom AI tutors is best modelled as
a shared resource between student and tutor, not a property of the
student alone."
  4. 4

    Justification & Reasoning

    Goal: Build the argument that your claim is more useful than the alternatives.

    What to include

    • Logical steps from assumptions to conclusions.
    • Empirical findings from prior work that your model explains.
    • Phenomena that competing theories struggle with but yours handles.
    • Honest statement of counter-examples and how you address them.

    Writing tip: treat every step as a potential failure point. If a step is weak, name it and defend it.

  5. 5

    Implications

    Goal: Show what follows if your claim is right — for research, practice, and future work.

    What to include

    • New empirical studies your model predicts.
    • How it changes the questions researchers should ask.
    • Practical consequences for designers, teachers, policymakers, etc.
    • Limits of the model and where it should not be applied.

    Writing tip: a good theoretical paper gives reviewers a testable prediction. Leave them with something falsifiable.

Common pitfalls

  • No central claim. If your paper's one sentence is "we discuss X", it is not theoretical yet.
  • Strawman rivals. Charity to opposing views is what makes your argument credible.
  • Borrowed jargon. Define every technical term you introduce.
  • Untethered speculation. Each claim should cite prior work, formal reasoning, or a stated assumption.
  • No falsifiability. If nothing could ever prove you wrong, you have a worldview, not a theory.

How to use LitLab as you write

  • Use Search New Papers to collect the key theoretical pieces you will argue with.
  • Create collections per rival view, so your existing-theories section writes itself from your notes.
  • Keep a short note on every paper saying what assumption this paper makes — your argument often attacks assumptions, not findings.
  • Draft your one-sentence claim first, then iterate it as the literature teaches you new nuance.
