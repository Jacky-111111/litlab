IMRAD

Write an IMRAD paper

IMRAD (Introduction, Methods, Results, Discussion) is the standard shape of an empirical research paper. Use it when you collected or analyzed data and want to report what you found.

When to use

You ran an experiment, survey, or data study.

Typical length

4,000 – 8,000 words

Key move

Answer one clear question with evidence.

The big idea

An IMRAD paper tells a tight, linear story: you ask a question, explain how you answered it, show what happened, and interpret what it means. Each section has one job. Keep them separate — results describe data, discussion interprets it.

Section-by-section walkthrough

  1. 1

    Introduction

    Goal: Convince the reader that your question matters, and state it clearly.

    What to include

    • Context and background of the problem.
    • What previous researchers have already shown.
    • The gap or unanswered question you are targeting.
    • Your specific research question or hypothesis.

    Writing tip: funnel from broad context → narrow gap → your one question. Don't summarize everything you read. 

    Example
"Despite rising interest in climate migration, most studies focus on
adult populations. It is unclear how K-12 curricula introduce the
topic. We ask: how do US high-school textbooks frame climate
migration between 2010 and 2024?"
  2. 2

    Methods

    Goal: Let another researcher reproduce exactly what you did.

    What to include

    • Data source(s) and how you selected them.
    • Inclusion / exclusion rules.
    • Variables, measurements, or coding scheme.
    • Tools, software, statistical tests.
    • Ethics / consent, if human subjects are involved.

    Writing tip: write Methods in past tense. If a grader could not replicate your study from this section alone, it is missing something.

  3. 3

    Results

    Goal: Describe what you found — without interpreting it yet.

    What to include

    • Headline findings stated in plain language.
    • Descriptive stats or counts before inferential tests.
    • Figures and tables that each answer one sub-question.
    • Effect sizes or uncertainty (p-values alone are not enough).

    Writing tip: do not use words like "surprisingly" or "importantly" here — that belongs in Discussion.

  4. 4

    Discussion

    Goal: Explain what your results mean, how they connect to prior work, and their limits.

    What to include

    • A clear answer to your research question.
    • How findings confirm, extend, or challenge prior work.
    • Honest limitations — don't hide them.
    • Practical or theoretical implications.
    • Specific future work.

    Writing tip: every claim here must trace back to something in Results. If it doesn't, you are speculating.

Common pitfalls

  • Mixing results and interpretation. Keep them in separate sections.
  • No clear research question. If a reader cannot quote your question after Introduction, rewrite it.
  • Methods without reproducibility. Name your tools, datasets, versions, and filters.
  • Cherry-picking results. Report findings that weaken your hypothesis too.
  • Over-claiming. Scope your conclusions to what your data actually shows.

How to use LitLab as you write

  • Use Search New Papers to collect background reading for your Introduction.
  • Save them to a collection so you can cite the same sources across the project.
  • For each paper, use LitLab's AI overview and key-terms features to quickly decide whether it belongs in your related work.
  • In your project workspace, keep a short note per paper: why is this cited, and in which section?
