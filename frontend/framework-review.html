Review / Survey

Write a Review or Survey paper

A Review paper maps what research already exists on a topic, synthesizes it, and shows where the gaps are. You are a cartographer of the field, not an experimenter.

When to use

You want to understand a field before running new work.

Typical length

4,000 – 10,000 words

Key move

Organize other people's findings into a clear story.

The big idea

A good review does three things: collects the relevant papers, organizes them into meaningful categories, and synthesizes patterns across them. A bad review is just a list of summaries. Your job is to argue that the field looks a certain way and show the evidence.

Section-by-section walkthrough

  1. 1

    Background & Scope

    Goal: Set the topic, define terms, and draw the boundary of what you will (and will not) cover.

    What to include

    • Why the topic matters now.
    • Definitions of your core concepts.
    • The exact question(s) your review answers.
    • Time range, disciplines, languages, or venues included.

    Writing tip: a reader should be able to predict which papers would be in scope and which would not after reading this section.

  2. 2

    Search Method

    Goal: Show you did not just pick the papers that agree with you.

    What to include

    • Databases used (Google Scholar, ACM, PubMed, etc.).
    • Search terms and boolean queries.
    • Inclusion / exclusion criteria.
    • How many papers you screened vs. kept — a PRISMA-style diagram helps.

    Writing tip: even a small student review benefits from listing your search terms — it turns opinion into method.

  3. 3

    Themes / Categories

    Goal: Group the papers into a small number of meaningful buckets.

    What to include

    • 3–6 themes that together cover the literature.
    • A short description of each theme.
    • Which papers belong to each theme (a table works well).

    Writing tip: themes should not overlap heavily. If two themes share most of the same papers, merge them. 

    Example themes for "LLMs in education"
1. Tutoring & feedback systems
2. Assessment & academic integrity
3. Curriculum design
4. Teacher workload & workflow
  4. 4

    Synthesis

    Goal: Compare findings across papers — don't summarize them one by one.

    What to include

    • Where studies agree and where they conflict.
    • How methods differ and how that might explain conflicts.
    • What is well-established vs. still contested.

    Writing tip: whenever you cite a paper, ask why here? If you couldn't cite someone else making the same move, you are probably just summarizing.

  5. 5

    Gaps & Future Work

    Goal: Explain what is missing and what should be studied next.

    What to include

    • Questions no paper has answered.
    • Methodological weaknesses across the field.
    • Concrete, actionable future research directions.

    Writing tip: "more research is needed" is not a gap. Describe what kind of study would close it.

Common pitfalls

  • Annotated bibliography, not a review. Don't go paper-by-paper; organize by theme.
  • No defined scope. Readers cannot judge completeness if they don't know your boundary.
  • Invisible search process. Say how you found the papers, not just what you found.
  • Avoiding conflicts. Tensions between studies make a review interesting; surface them.
  • Vague gap statements. Point at specific missing methods, populations, or questions.

How to use LitLab as you write

  • Use Search New Papers repeatedly with different search terms — capture the process.
  • Create one collection per theme, so your taxonomy lives in the app, not just your head.
  • Tag each paper with a 1-line reason you kept it — that note becomes the backbone of your synthesis.
  • Use LitLab's AI overview to triage quickly, but always read the methods section yourself before citing.
