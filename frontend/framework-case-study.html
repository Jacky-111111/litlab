Case Study

Write a Case Study paper

A case study zooms in on one (or a few) real-world instance and studies it in depth. You are not trying to prove a general law; you are trying to understand a specific context so thoroughly that others can learn from it.

When to use

You have rich access to one situation and want to learn from it.

Typical length

3,000 – 7,000 words

Key move

Describe the case faithfully, then extract transferable lessons.

The big idea

Good case studies are narrow but deep. Depth comes from multiple sources of evidence (documents, interviews, artifacts, observations) triangulated together. Your job is to explain not only what happened but why — in this specific setting.

Section-by-section walkthrough

  1. 1

    Case & Context

    Goal: Describe the specific situation you are studying and why it is interesting.

    What to include

    • What the case is (an organization, event, system, person…).
    • Setting: time, place, institutional context.
    • Why this case is representative, unusual, or informative.
    • Ethical considerations and anonymization approach.

    Writing tip: name the type of case — exemplar, outlier, revelatory, or longitudinal — so readers know what kind of insight to expect.

  2. 2

    Research Question

    Goal: State the specific question the case is supposed to answer.

    What to include

    • A narrow question that the case can answer.
    • Links to the broader theoretical or practical issue it speaks to.

    Writing tip: case studies answer "how" and "why" questions well; they answer "how often" questions badly. Pick your question accordingly. 

    Example question
"How did one mid-sized public library integrate generative AI into its
reference services in 2024–2025, and which organizational factors
shaped the outcome?"
  3. 3

    Method (Data Collection)

    Goal: Explain exactly how you gathered your evidence.

    What to include

    • Data sources: interviews, documents, observations, logs, artifacts.
    • How many of each, over what time period.
    • Coding scheme or analytical approach (thematic analysis, grounded theory, narrative analysis…).
    • How you handled bias — your role in the case, member checking, triangulation.

    Writing tip: show at least two independent data sources. A case study built only on interviews is easy to critique.

  4. 4

    Findings

    Goal: Tell the story of the case with evidence woven in.

    What to include

    • Key themes that emerged from the data.
    • Representative quotes, excerpts, or artifacts — anonymized.
    • A timeline if events unfold over time.
    • Tensions or surprises in the case.

    Writing tip: every theme should be anchored in multiple data points. One quote is anecdote; three is a pattern.

  5. 5

    Lessons Learned

    Goal: Turn the story into insight that travels beyond this one case.

    What to include

    • What this case teaches about the research question.
    • Generalizable principles — stated carefully, with scope.
    • Practical recommendations for similar contexts.
    • Limits: what this case cannot tell you.

    Writing tip: a single case cannot prove a law. Write "this case suggests…" not "we have shown that…".

Common pitfalls

  • Over-generalizing. One case gives depth, not universality.
  • Descriptive only. Description without analysis is a report, not research.
  • Single data source. Reviewers expect triangulation.
  • Missing context. Without the setting, readers cannot judge transferability.
  • Hidden researcher role. If you were embedded in the case, say so — and reflect on bias.

How to use LitLab as you write

  • Collect background papers about similar cases in a dedicated collection — they become your comparison baseline.
  • For each paper, note the method used. Case studies are often critiqued on methodology, so you want examples to defend your choices.
  • Use Search New Papers to find theoretical lenses (e.g. organizational change, technology adoption) that help you interpret your case.
  • Keep field notes outside LitLab, but keep all published evidence and theoretical references here.
